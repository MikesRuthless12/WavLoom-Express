# Go-to-Market — SampleMint

## 1. Market Context

The music production tools market is worth over $4 billion globally, with bedroom producers and independent creators driving a growing share of that spend. The "creator economy" shift has turned beat-making from a niche hobby into a legitimate income path — platforms like BeatStars processed over $200 million in creator payouts, and sample pack sales on Splice, Gumroad, and Loopmasters represent a significant secondary revenue stream for producers at every level.

Three converging trends make SampleMint's timing sharp. First, AI-generated audio has exploded in popularity but created a copyright crisis — producers are generating sounds they legally can't sell, and the smarter ones are looking for alternatives. Second, the sample pack market is oversaturated with recycled content — every producer has the same Splice kits, and differentiation is increasingly valuable. Third, the "sell your own kits" economy is booming — producers who once only sold beats are now selling drum kits, loop packs, and presets as a major side income. The tools to create those kits haven't kept up with the demand.

SampleMint enters this gap at exactly the right moment: producers need original sounds, they need to own them, and they need to create them fast. The market is educated enough to understand the value proposition but underserved enough that no tool directly addresses it.

## 2. Launch Strategy

**Phase 1: Pre-launch (Weeks -8 to -1)**
Build anticipation and assemble a beta testing group. The goal is to have 100 producers aware of SampleMint and 20–30 committed beta testers before the soft launch. All activity centers on two channels: YouTube producer outreach and Discord community building. No paid advertising — the budget doesn't support it, and organic credibility matters more to this audience.

**Phase 2: Soft launch (Weeks 1–2)**
Release SampleMint as a free beta to the testing group. The goal is to validate the core workflow (analyze → design → export), collect feedback on sound quality and UX, and identify critical bugs before public launch. Beta testers get lifetime premium access as a thank-you — this creates evangelists and gives them incentive to provide thorough feedback.

**Phase 3: Public launch (Week 3)**
Release SampleMint publicly with the freemium model active. Launch simultaneously on the SampleMint website, YouTube (demo video), and Discord. Coordinate with YouTube producers who received early access to publish their reviews during launch week. The goal is 500 downloads in the first 30 days.

## 3. Pre-Launch Playbook

**Week -8 to -7: Foundation**
Set up the SampleMint Discord server with channels: #announcements, #general, #feedback, #sound-design-tips, #show-your-kits, and #preset-sharing. Design the server with the SampleMint brand (dark theme, lime green accents where Discord allows). Write a pinned welcome message that explains what SampleMint is and what the community is for. Create a simple landing page at samplemint.com with the tagline "Your sound. Your copyright," a one-paragraph description, and an email signup form for launch notifications. Use a simple static page — no need for a full website yet.

**Week -6 to -5: Community seeding**
Join 5–8 active beat-making Discord servers (look for servers with 500+ members focused on Trap, Drill, EDM, and general production). Don't promote SampleMint immediately. Instead, show up with value: answer questions about sound design, share tips on creating original drum sounds, engage in conversations about selling kits. Build a reputation as someone who knows their stuff. The goal is 20+ genuine interactions per week. Simultaneously, identify 10–15 mid-tier YouTube producers (10k–100k subscribers) who create content about beat-making, sound design, or kit creation. Watch their recent videos and note what topics they cover.

**Week -4 to -3: Outreach**
Reach out to the 10–15 identified YouTubers via email or DM. The pitch is simple: "I'm building a tool that lets producers create original, copyrightable drum kits and one-shots — no AI, no recycled samples. It's free to try. Would you be interested in early access before public launch?" Don't send a mass template — personalize each message by referencing a specific video they made. Target a 30% response rate (3–5 interested creators). For those who respond, send them a beta build and a one-page overview of SampleMint's key features. In the Discord servers, start softly introducing SampleMint: "I've been working on something for producers who want to create original kits — here's a sneak peek of the waveform analysis." Share a screenshot or short screen recording. Direct interested people to the SampleMint Discord.

**Week -2 to -1: Beta prep**
Finalize the beta build. Create a simple feedback form (Google Forms or Typeform) covering: sound quality rating (1–5), workflow ease (1–5), features used, bugs encountered, and open comments. Send the beta build to all confirmed testers (YouTubers + Discord early adopters). Post in the SampleMint Discord: "Beta is live. Download link in #announcements. Break things and tell me about it." Record a 3–5 minute "SampleMint walkthrough" video for your own YouTube channel showing the analyze → design → export flow. Don't over-produce it — a clean screen recording with voiceover matches the brand's no-nonsense personality.

## 4. Launch Week Plan

**Monday (Day 1):** Publish the walkthrough video on YouTube. Title: "I built a tool that lets you create copyrightable drum kits in minutes." Push the public release build to samplemint.com. Announce in the SampleMint Discord. Post in 3–4 beat-making Discord servers where you've been active (not spamming — share it naturally in relevant conversations about sound design or kit creation).

**Tuesday (Day 2):** If any YouTube creators have published their reviews, share those in the Discord and on Twitter/X. Engage with every comment on your YouTube video. Monitor download numbers and any crash reports. Fix critical issues immediately.

**Wednesday (Day 3):** Share a "Day 2 numbers" update in the Discord: download count, first user feedback quotes, any cool kits people have made. This transparency builds community trust. Post a short-form video clip (30–60 seconds) on Instagram/TikTok showing the A/B comparison feature — the visual of waveform comparison is compelling on camera.

**Thursday (Day 4):** Reach out to music production blogs and newsletters with a press-ready description and download link. Target: BeatButter, Magnetic Magazine, MusicTech, ProducerHive. Don't expect coverage immediately — plant seeds for the coming weeks.

**Friday (Day 5):** Post a "Week 1 recap" in the Discord. Share honest numbers, user feedback (good and bad), and what you're fixing or improving next. Announce the first "community challenge": create a 5-sound drum kit using SampleMint and share it in #show-your-kits. Best kit gets featured on the SampleMint YouTube channel.

**Weekend:** Rest. Monitor crash reports. Respond to Discord questions. Don't push content — let the launch breathe.

## 5. Post-Launch Growth

**Weeks 1–4: Listen and iterate.**
The first month is about learning, not scaling. Monitor which features people use most (single analysis vs. presets, which effects, export count per session). Track where people get stuck — if first-time users aren't exporting within 5 minutes, the UX needs work. Push 2–3 small updates fixing the most common pain points. Share each update in the Discord with a brief changelog. Ask beta testers to update their reviews if issues they flagged are fixed.

**Weeks 5–8: Content engine.**
Start publishing a weekly YouTube video. Alternate between two formats: (1) "Sound design walkthrough" — take a specific sound (a hard trap kick, a lo-fi snare, an EDM lead) and show how to create it in SampleMint from analysis or preset, and (2) "Kit building session" — build a complete themed kit on camera in real time. Each video ends with "Download SampleMint free at samplemint.com." Share clips from these videos on Instagram Reels and TikTok. Cross-post to the Discord.

**Weeks 9–12: Community flywheel.**
By now, early users should be creating content with SampleMint. Amplify them: repost their kit showcases, feature their workflows on the SampleMint YouTube channel, highlight their presets in the Discord. Run a second community challenge with a bigger incentive (e.g., 1 year of premium free). Start tracking which user-generated content drives the most downloads and double down on that type. Introduce the premium tier if it hasn't been gated yet — soft-launch it with a discount for early adopters (e.g., 30% off for the first 100 buyers).

## 6. Channel Strategy

**1. YouTube (highest ROI)**
Expected effort: 4–6 hours/week (1 video + clips). Expected return: highest single-channel driver of downloads. Timeline: 4–8 weeks to see consistent traffic. Why it works: producers learn by watching. A screen recording of the SampleMint workflow is inherently compelling — the waveform visuals, the knob adjustments, the A/B comparison all translate perfectly to video. This is your primary growth channel.

**2. Discord (highest retention)**
Expected effort: 1–2 hours/day for community management. Expected return: best channel for retention, feedback, and word of mouth. Timeline: immediate. Why it works: producers live on Discord. A dedicated server becomes the hub for preset sharing, feedback, and peer-to-peer support. Active communities create stickiness that no other channel can match.

**3. YouTube creator partnerships (highest leverage)**
Expected effort: 2–3 hours/week for outreach and relationship management. Expected return: a single review from a 50k-subscriber producer can drive 200–500 downloads. Timeline: 2–6 weeks from outreach to published video. Why it works: producers trust other producers. A genuine review from someone they follow is the most powerful form of marketing for this audience.

**4. Beat-making Discord servers (best for awareness)**
Expected effort: 30 minutes/day engaging in conversations. Expected return: slow burn awareness that compounds over time. Timeline: 2–4 weeks to build reputation, ongoing. Why it works: you're meeting producers where they already are. Not selling — showing up with knowledge and letting SampleMint come up naturally in context.

**5. Instagram / TikTok short-form (best for viral moments)**
Expected effort: 1–2 hours/week creating clips from YouTube content. Expected return: unpredictable — one viral clip can drive thousands of downloads, or months can pass with modest traction. Timeline: variable. Why it works: the SampleMint UI is visually striking (lime green waveforms on dark background), and the A/B comparison is inherently shareable. Short-form is high-upside, low-effort when repurposing existing video content.

## 7. Content Strategy

**Core content format: YouTube workflow videos (weekly)**
Every video follows the same structure: (1) Start with the end result — play the finished sound or kit. (2) Show the process — load the reference or preset, tweak knobs, toggle modes. (3) A/B compare — the visual and audible proof of originality. (4) Export and show the file on disk. Videos should be 5–10 minutes, no intro fluff, no "smash that subscribe button." The brand is direct and no-nonsense — the content should be too.

**Secondary content: short-form clips (3–4x/week)**
Pull the most visually compelling 30–60 second segments from YouTube videos. The waveform analysis moment, a dramatic A/B comparison, a before/after effects adjustment. These go on Instagram Reels and TikTok. Caption format: direct, minimal. "Made this kick in 45 seconds." "Original vs. mine. Which is which?" No hashtag spam — use 3–5 relevant tags max.

**Tertiary content: Discord-first tips**
Share quick sound design tips in the Discord that don't warrant a full video. "Pro tip: start with the filter fully open and pull it down to find the sweet spot, rather than starting closed and opening up." These establish expertise and keep the community engaged between video drops.

**Content calendar cadence:**
Monday — YouTube video publish. Tuesday–Thursday — 1 short-form clip/day on Instagram and TikTok. Friday — Discord community tip or challenge. This is sustainable for a solo founder spending 6–8 hours/week on content.

## 8. Community Strategy

**Home base: SampleMint Discord server.**
This is not a support channel — it's a creative community. The channel structure encourages sharing and collaboration: #show-your-kits for showcasing work, #preset-sharing for trading user-created presets (post-MVP), #feedback for product input, #sound-design-tips for technique discussion. Mike should be visibly active daily — not just moderating, but participating in sound design conversations, sharing his own work, and responding to kit showcases with genuine feedback.

**Existing communities to participate in (not promote in):**
Show up consistently in 3–5 established beat-making Discord servers. The ones with 500–5,000 members tend to be more engaged than massive servers. Look for servers focused on Trap, Drill, EDM, and FL Studio/Ableton production. The approach is always value-first: answer questions, share knowledge, build relationships. SampleMint comes up when it's relevant, not as a pitch. "Yeah, I actually built a tool for this — here's how it works" in response to a question about creating original kits is authentic. "Check out my new app SampleMint!" posted in #general is spam.

**Community milestones to celebrate:**
50 Discord members, 100 members, first user-created kit uploaded, first kit sold on BeatStars that was made with SampleMint, first YouTube mention by an outside creator. Each milestone gets a brief, genuine celebration post — not a marketing moment.

## 9. Key Metrics

**Acquisition:**
Downloads per week — Target: 50/week by week 4, 100/week by week 8. Track by platform (macOS, Windows, Linux).
Website visitors — Target: 500/week by week 4. Track source (YouTube, Discord, direct, organic search).
YouTube video views — Target: 500 views/video in first week.

**Activation:**
Time to first export — Target: under 5 minutes. If this is above 10 minutes, the onboarding is failing.
First-session export rate — Target: 60%+ of users who open the app export at least one sound.

**Retention:**
7-day return rate — Target: 40%. Producers who come back within a week found real value.
30-day return rate — Target: 25%. These are becoming regular users.
Weekly active exporters — Target: 30 at 90 days (the North Star metric from product-vision.md).

**Revenue:**
Premium conversion rate — Target: 2–5% of total users.
Monthly recurring revenue (if subscription) or total revenue (if one-time) — Target: 10 paying customers at 90 days.
Average revenue per paying user — Track to ensure pricing is in the right range.

**Community:**
Discord member count — Target: 100 at 90 days.
Discord daily active users — Target: 20% of members active daily.
User-generated content pieces (kits shared, presets shared, YouTube mentions) — Track for community health.

## 10. Budget Considerations

Mike is bootstrapping on a $50–100/month total budget. Here's how to allocate it.

**Non-negotiable costs:**
VPS for license server: $5–6/month (DigitalOcean or Hetzner).
Domain name (samplemint.com): ~$12/year (~$1/month amortized).
Apple Developer Program (for macOS code signing): $99/year (~$8/month amortized).

**Total fixed costs: ~$15/month** — leaving $35–85/month for discretionary spending.

**Recommended discretionary allocation:**
Backup storage (DigitalOcean Spaces or Backblaze B2): $5/month.
Email service for transactional emails (license key delivery, if not fully handled by Polar): $0 (Polar handles delivery) or $5/month for a service like Resend if custom emails are needed.
Screen recording software for YouTube content: $0 (OBS Studio is free).
Simple landing page hosting: $0 (Vercel free tier or GitHub Pages).

**What costs $0 that might seem expensive:**
YouTube channel — free. Discord server — free. GitHub repository — free. GitHub Actions CI/CD — free tier covers the build minutes needed. Let's Encrypt SSL — free. Google Fonts — free. Tailwind CSS — free. OBS for screen recording — free.

**What to avoid spending on:**
Paid ads — the budget doesn't support meaningful ad spend, and organic credibility matters more to this audience. Premium design tools — use Figma free tier or skip formal design and iterate in code. Paid PR services — direct outreach to YouTube creators is more effective and free. Premium analytics tools — start with simple tracking (Plausible at $9/month if budget allows, otherwise wait).

**When to increase spending:**
When premium revenue exceeds infrastructure costs (roughly 5–10 paying customers), reinvest the margin. First priority: better hosting or analytics. Second priority: small sponsorship of a YouTube creator's video ($50–200 per mention). Don't increase spending on infrastructure ahead of demand.

## 11. Risks

**Risk: Sound quality doesn't sell itself on video.**
The entire GTM strategy assumes that seeing the SampleMint workflow on YouTube is compelling. If the waveform visuals are unremarkable or the exported sounds don't impress listeners, YouTube content won't convert. Mitigation: invest time in making the demo sounds excellent before recording any content. The first YouTube video sets the quality bar — don't rush it.

**Risk: YouTube creator partnerships don't materialize.**
Mid-tier producers may not respond to outreach, or their reviews may be lukewarm. A 10–20% response rate is realistic for cold outreach, and not every review will be enthusiastic. Mitigation: cast a wider net (15–20 creators, not 5), personalize every message, and be prepared to rely on your own YouTube channel as the primary content engine if partnerships are slow.

**Risk: Discord community doesn't reach critical mass.**
A Discord server with 10 members feels dead. If the community doesn't grow past 50 members in the first 2 months, it becomes a liability rather than an asset. Mitigation: seed the community with engaged beta testers before public launch. Be the most active member for the first 3 months. Run regular challenges and events to create reasons to visit. If the Discord stalls, consider consolidating into a single #samplemint channel within an existing popular production Discord instead.

**Risk: Free tier is too generous — nobody upgrades.**
If the free tier covers everything a casual producer needs, conversion to premium will be near zero. The core workflow (analyze, design, export single sounds) may be sufficient for most users. Mitigation: monitor what percentage of users attempt to access premium-gated features (kit builder, batch analysis). If fewer than 10% of active users hit the gate, consider moving the gate earlier in the workflow (e.g., limit free exports to 5/day).

**Risk: The "copyright" message doesn't resonate.**
The copyright angle is a key differentiator, but many producers may not care — they sell kits without thinking about copyright and haven't been burned. The pain point may be real but latent. Mitigation: lead with workflow speed and sound quality in marketing, not copyright. Use copyright as a secondary differentiator for the producers who do care. Test messaging variants in YouTube video titles to see what drives more clicks.

**Risk: Cross-platform bugs undermine trust at launch.**
Electron apps can behave differently across macOS, Windows, and Linux. A crash or audio glitch on launch day — especially in a YouTube creator's review — could poison first impressions. Mitigation: beta test aggressively on all three platforms before public launch. Prioritize Windows (largest audience) and macOS (second largest) for bug fixes. Ship Linux as "beta" if needed.

**Risk: Solo founder burnout.**
Building the product, creating content, managing the community, handling support, and doing outreach — all as one person — is a recipe for exhaustion. Mitigation: protect creative energy by batching content creation (record 2–3 videos in one session). Set a sustainable pace: 2 hours/day on community, 6 hours/day on product, 2 hours/day on content. Take weekends off from community management when possible. Recruit a Discord moderator from the beta tester group early.
