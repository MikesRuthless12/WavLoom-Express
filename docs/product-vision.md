# Product Vision — SampleMint

## 1. Vision & Mission

### Vision Statement

Every music producer owns a library of sounds that are unmistakably theirs — original, copyrightable, and built from their own creative decisions — regardless of their budget, gear, or technical background.

### Mission Statement

SampleMint gives producers the fastest path from reference audio or raw presets to original, export-ready one-shots and drum kits they fully own and can sell commercially.

### Founder's Why

Mike is a Dark Trap producer who has spent years sitting down to make beats and hitting the same wall: wanting original sounds he could own and sell, but finding that every available option either sounded fake, cost too much time, or left him in a copyright gray zone. He's lived the frustration of browsing Splice for the 500th time, knowing the same hi-hats showing up in his tracks are showing up in everyone else's too.

What makes Mike different from most people complaining about this problem is that he can actually fix it. With a Master's in Information Technology and years of production experience, he sits at the exact intersection of the two worlds that audio tool companies consistently fail to bridge: engineering and beat-making. Most audio tools are built by engineers who don't produce, or by producers who can't code. Mike is both.

He also understands the business side of production in a way that shapes every product decision. He knows producers don't just make music for fun — they sell beats, kits, and sample packs as a livelihood. He understands the economics of BeatStars, Splice, and Gumroad because he lives in that ecosystem. SampleMint isn't a creative toy. It's a revenue tool, built by someone who gets it, priced so that every producer — not just the ones with Grammy budgets — can afford it.

### Core Values

**Ownership is non-negotiable.** Every sound that leaves SampleMint is the producer's intellectual property, period. The entire product is designed around this principle: producers make the creative decisions, the tool provides the starting point and controls, and the exported result is theirs to copyright and sell. No legal gray areas, no asterisks.

**Respect the producer's time.** A producer's creative energy is finite. Every click, every screen, every interaction must earn its place. If something takes 10 clicks and it could take 3, that's a bug. If a workflow breaks creative flow, that's a failure. SampleMint treats attention as the most valuable resource.

**Price like a producer, not a corporation.** Affordability isn't a nice-to-have — it's a core value. SampleMint is priced so that a bedroom producer making $200/month from beats can justify it. If only established producers can afford the tool, it has failed its mission.

**Ship the weapon, not the toy.** SampleMint is a professional tool for people who make money with their music. Every design decision, every feature, every word of copy should reflect that. No gamification, no fluff, no hand-holding where it isn't needed.

**Sound quality is the product.** If the exported sounds don't pass the "would I actually use this in a beat?" test, nothing else matters. Ultra-realistic output isn't a feature — it's the entire value proposition. Every instrument preset, every synthesis parameter, and every effect must meet a studio-quality bar.

### Strategic Pillars

**Local-first, cloud-later.** All audio processing happens on-device. Zero latency, full offline capability, no subscription-gated core features. Cloud infrastructure exists only for licensing. This keeps costs predictable and the product fast.

**Two entry points, one destination.** Whether a producer starts from a reference file or a preset, they end up in the same place: a sound they designed, they own, and they can sell. Both paths are first-class citizens.

**The producer's workflow comes first.** Every technical decision — from the knob layout to the drag-and-drop DAW integration — is made from the perspective of "how does a producer actually work?" not "what's easiest to implement?"

**Accessible doesn't mean dumbed down.** The interface should be immediately usable by a beginner, but the depth of control should satisfy a power user. Clean layout with no unnecessary controls, but every control that's there gives real creative power.

### Success Looks Like

In 12 months, SampleMint has 10,000+ users across macOS, Windows, and Linux, with a passionate community of 1,000+ active Discord members sharing presets and kits. Multiple mid-tier YouTube producers have featured it in tutorials, and beat-making educators are building courses around it. 800+ producers are on the premium tier, generating enough revenue to cover infrastructure costs and fund full-time development. Producers are tagging #MadeWithSampleMint on their BeatStars kit listings. The preset library has expanded significantly through community contributions. Internationalization has opened up markets in Latin America, Europe, and Asia. Most importantly, producers who use SampleMint have stopped buying sample packs and started selling their own.

## 2. User Research

### Primary Persona

**Marcus**, 24, is a bedroom Trap producer in Atlanta. He sells beats on BeatStars for $30–$50 a lease and has been producing for 3 years. His mornings start with scrolling Splice for new sounds, his afternoons are spent making beats in FL Studio, and his evenings go to uploading content to YouTube and Instagram. He's been wanting to sell drum kits as a second income stream — he sees other producers pulling in $500–$2,000/month from kit sales — but he doesn't have the deep synthesis knowledge to design sounds from scratch. He knows he can't legally resell the Splice samples he's been using, so he's stuck. His tech comfort level is high for DAWs and plugins but low for sound design tools like Serum or Vital. He'd switch to a new tool the moment it lets him create original, sellable sounds without a steep learning curve. He's frustrated, resourceful, and motivated by the dream of making music full-time.

### Secondary Personas

Her days are spent in Ableton layering and processing sounds across 5–6 plugins to create original one-shots. The quality of her work is excellent, but the pipeline is painfully slow. She'd adopt SampleMint not to replace her skills but to accelerate her workflow — collapsing a multi-plugin chain into a single app that gets her from concept to export in minutes instead of hours.

**Deon**, 31, produces EDM and Drill and has 10k followers on Instagram. His priority is a signature sound — he's tired of hearing his exact same hi-hats in other producers' tracks. He wants to design sounds that are unmistakably his and eventually launch a branded kit line. SampleMint's A/B comparison feature is what would hook him — proof that his sounds are original is proof that his brand is real.

**Music educators and YouTube creators** use SampleMint as a teaching tool. They demo the analyze-and-recreate workflow in tutorials, which drives awareness and converts their students into SampleMint users. They care about clear visual feedback (waveform display, knob movements) that translates well on camera.

**Hobbyist producers** aren't selling yet but want to understand how sounds work. The preset library and visual waveform feedback make sound design approachable for the first time. They're tomorrow's power users and tomorrow's paying customers.

### Jobs To Be Done

**Functional jobs:** "When I want to create a drum kit to sell, I need a tool that lets me generate original sounds quickly so I can package and list them for sale without copyright risk." "When I hear a sound I like in someone else's beat, I need a way to analyze what makes it work and create my own version so I can use something similar without copying." "When I'm producing a beat, I need to be able to quickly audition and tweak sounds without leaving my creative flow so I don't lose the idea I'm working on."

**Emotional jobs:** "I want to feel like a creator, not a consumer — like I'm building something original, not just rearranging other people's work." "I want to feel confident that everything in my kit is legally mine so I can sell without anxiety." "I want to feel like my beats have a sonic identity that's uniquely mine."

**Social jobs:** "I want to be known as a producer who makes original sounds, not one who uses the same packs as everyone else." "I want to sell kits that other producers respect and want to buy because they know the sounds are unique." "I want to be seen as a serious professional, not a hobbyist using recycled samples."

### Pain Points

**Copyright uncertainty (severity: critical, frequency: constant).** Producers using AI generation tools or recycled samples live with a constant background anxiety about whether they can legally sell what they've made. Most don't research the legal implications — they just hope for the best. The consequence is building a revenue stream on a foundation that could collapse with a single DMCA claim. Currently, producers either ignore the risk or avoid selling kits entirely.

**No single-tool pipeline (severity: high, frequency: every session).** Creating an original one-shot today requires chaining 3–5 tools: a DAW for arrangement, a synth for sound generation, effects plugins for processing, a separate app for waveform analysis, and a file manager for export and organization. Each context switch breaks creative flow. Currently, producers just accept this friction as "how it works."

**Recycled sound identity (severity: high, frequency: ongoing).** Using the same sample packs as everyone else means producers' beats sound like everyone else's beats. This is particularly painful for producers trying to build a brand or a following — their "signature sound" isn't a signature if 10,000 other producers have the same hi-hats. Currently, the only solution is deep synthesis knowledge that most producers don't have.

**Prohibitive tool cost (severity: medium, frequency: ongoing).** Splice subscriptions ($10–$30/month), synth plugins ($100–$300 each), sample pack purchases ($15–$50 each) — it adds up, especially for bedroom producers who might only be making $200–$500/month from beats. Currently, producers either overspend or settle for free/low-quality options.

**Steep sound design learning curve (severity: medium, frequency: at point of entry).** Tools like Serum and Vital are powerful but require significant time investment to learn. A producer who just wants a custom kick drum shouldn't need to understand wavetable synthesis, FM modulation, and envelope design. Currently, most producers simply don't attempt original sound design.

### Current Alternatives & Competitive Landscape

**DAW plugin chains (Ableton + Serum/Vital + effects):** The current gold standard for original sound design. Produces high-quality results if you have the knowledge. Falls short because it requires deep synthesis expertise, the workflow is fragmented across multiple tools, and it's painfully slow for batch production. Switching cost is low in theory (SampleMint complements rather than replaces the DAW) but high in habit — producers are used to their workflow even if it's inefficient.

**AI generation tools (Splice Create, Drumloop AI, various AI sample generators):** Fast and easy — type a description, get a sound. Falls short because copyright ownership is unclear, output quality is inconsistent, and you can't fine-tune results. Producers who care about owning their sounds avoid these entirely. Switching cost is essentially zero since these tools don't build deep user investment.

**Sample marketplaces (Splice, Cymatics, Loopmasters, BeatStars kits):** Huge libraries, instant access, professional quality. Falls short because you don't own the sounds — you license them, often with restrictions on resale. Everyone has access to the same pools, destroying uniqueness. Switching cost is moderate — producers have invested in subscriptions and downloaded libraries they're familiar with.

**Manual workarounds ("just pitch it and call it original"):** Some producers grab a sample, pitch-shift it, add reverb, and claim it as their own. Falls short because it's legally questionable, the results are obviously derivative, and it doesn't scale. Switching cost is zero.

**"Just living with it":** Many producers accept they can't create original sounds and either avoid selling kits or sell them hoping nobody notices. Falls short because it caps their revenue potential and creates ongoing legal anxiety. Switching cost is zero — these producers are the most eager to adopt a real solution.

### Key Assumptions to Validate

**Assumption 1:** Producers will pay for a standalone sound design tool separate from their DAW. Test by running a pre-launch survey in beat-making Discord servers asking producers what they'd pay for a tool that creates copyrightable one-shots. If fewer than 30% express willingness to pay, the freemium model needs to carry more weight.

**Assumption 2:** The A/B comparison feature is a meaningful differentiator, not just a novelty. Test by putting A/B comparison in the hands of 20 beta users and tracking whether they use it beyond the first session. If usage drops off, it's a demo feature, not a workflow feature.

**Assumption 3:** Ultra-realistic preset sounds can be achieved using existing audio processing libraries without building a custom synthesis engine from scratch. Test by prototyping the core sound engine early (Phase 1) and having 10 producers evaluate output quality. If realism falls short, the entire value proposition needs rethinking.

**Assumption 4:** Producers actually care about copyright when selling drum kits. Test by surveying 50+ producers on BeatStars about whether copyright concerns have ever affected their kit-selling decisions. It's possible most producers don't think about this and the pain point is less acute than assumed.

**Assumption 5:** Batch directory analysis is a real workflow need, not an edge case. Test by tracking how often beta users use single-file vs. directory analysis. If 80%+ use single-file only, the batch feature can be deprioritized.

**Assumption 6:** The learning curve for SampleMint's knob-based controls is low enough for producers without sound design experience. Test with 5 producers who have never used a synthesizer and observe whether they can create an export-ready sound in under 5 minutes without guidance.

**Assumption 7:** Drag-and-drop DAW integration works reliably across FL Studio, Ableton, Logic, and other major DAWs on all three operating systems. Test early in development — if cross-DAW drag-and-drop is unreliable, it needs to be flagged as a known limitation.

## 3. Product Strategy

### Product Principles

**One app, start to finish.** A producer should never need to leave SampleMint to complete any step of the sound design → kit building → export workflow. The moment they have to open another tool, something is wrong.

**Show, don't tell.** The waveform is the truth. When a producer turns a knob, the waveform responds in real time. When they A/B compare, they see and hear the difference. Visual feedback replaces guesswork.

**Fast enough to keep up with creativity.** Sound design in SampleMint should feel as immediate as playing an instrument. Analysis should be near-instant. Knob adjustments should produce audible results with zero perceptible delay. Export should take seconds, not minutes.

**Two doors, same house.** The analyze-a-reference path and the start-from-preset path are equally polished, equally deep, and lead to the same destination: an original sound the producer owns. Neither is a second-class citizen.

**The export is the product.** Everything in SampleMint exists to serve the quality of the final exported audio file. If a feature doesn't improve the export or the path to it, it doesn't belong.

**Accessible on day one, deep on day thirty.** A first-time user exports a sound in under 5 minutes. A power user discovers new techniques months later. The surface is simple; the depth is real.

### Market Differentiation

SampleMint occupies a unique position in the audio tool landscape because it's the only product designed specifically for the workflow of producers who sell sounds. Existing tools fall into three camps: DAW-based workflows that are powerful but fragmented and slow, AI generators that are fast but legally dubious, and sample marketplaces that give you sounds you can't truly own. SampleMint is none of these. It's a dedicated sound design workstation with a single purpose: getting original, copyrightable sounds out of a producer's head and into a sellable kit.

The A/B comparison feature is the clearest expression of this positioning. No other tool lets a producer put their output side-by-side with a reference and verify originality. This isn't just a feature — it's a statement about what SampleMint stands for. The kit builder and DAW drag-and-drop integration reinforce the same message: this tool was built by someone who sells beats, for people who sell beats.

The defensibility comes from three layers: first, the integrated pipeline (analyze → design → build kit → export) is hard to replicate piecemeal; second, the preset library grows with the community as users share presets; and third, the brand positioning as "the copyright-clear sound design tool" creates a narrative moat that competitors can't easily claim.

### Magic Moment Design

The magic moment is when a producer creates an original sound, A/B compares it with the reference, and realizes — with visual and audible proof — that they own something unique. For this to happen reliably, several things must be true in the product:

Analysis must be fast (under 3 seconds for a single file) and visually informative — the producer needs to see the waveform breakdown immediately. The knob controls must produce audible changes in real time with zero perceptible latency. The A/B comparison must be one click away, not buried in a menu. And the export must be instant — the producer shouldn't have time to second-guess before the file exists on their drive.

The shortest path to this moment from first launch: open app → pick a preset (or load a reference file) → adjust 2–3 knobs → hit A/B → export. This should be achievable in under 2 minutes. If the magic moment requires learning the interface first, the interface is wrong. Every element on the main screen should be self-explanatory for a producer who has used any audio plugin.

### MVP Definition

The MVP must be buildable in 6–8 weeks by a solo developer using Claude Code, and it must deliver the magic moment on first use.

**In Scope:**

**Audio file analysis engine** — Load a single audio file (WAV, MP3, FLAC), analyze its spectral characteristics, identify instrument type, and display the waveform. This is the foundation of the analyze-and-recreate workflow. Done when: a producer loads any supported audio file and sees an accurate waveform with analysis data within 3 seconds.

**Preset library (core instruments)** — A curated set of dry, ultra-realistic base sounds for the most common kit instruments: kicks, snares, hi-hats, claps, toms, 808s, plus a selection of melodic instruments (piano, flute, strings, brass). Done when: at least 30 presets are available across 10+ instrument categories, each sounding studio-quality with no effects applied.

**Knob-based effects controls** — Reverb, echo/delay, pitch, distortion/drive, filter (low-pass/high-pass), and volume. Each knob produces real-time audible changes with waveform visualization updating in sync. Done when: all 6 effect controls work with zero perceptible latency and visually update the waveform.

**Real-time waveform visualization** — Display the audio waveform that updates as the producer adjusts effects. Must be responsive and visually clear against the dark UI. Done when: waveform renders accurately, updates in real time during knob adjustments, and uses the lime green accent color.

**Realistic/human vs. EDM mode toggle** — A switch that shifts the synthesis and effects parameters between two sound profiles: natural/acoustic-sounding and electronic/processed. Done when: toggling the mode produces an audibly distinct character shift on any loaded sound.

**A/B comparison** — One-click toggle between the original reference audio and the producer's modified version. Visual waveform comparison side by side. Done when: a producer can instantly switch between original and modified audio with visual diff.

**Export to common formats** — Export the final sound as WAV (lossless, industry standard) and MP3 (for quick sharing). Done when: exported files play correctly in major DAWs and audio players with correct metadata.

**Built-in file explorer** — A panel for browsing local directories, loading audio files, and organizing output. Done when: producers can navigate their file system, load files by clicking, and see output files organized in their chosen export directory.

**Kit builder workspace** — A workspace where producers drag individual one-shots into slots to assemble a complete drum kit. Name the kit, export as a folder of organized files. Done when: a producer can build a kit from 8+ one-shots and export as a named, organized folder.

**Core Electron shell** — The cross-platform desktop app running on macOS, Windows, and Linux with proper window management, menu bar, and native feel. Done when: the app installs and runs cleanly on all three OSes.

### Explicitly Out of Scope

**Batch directory analysis** — Scanning an entire folder of files at once. This is a real workflow need (validated assumption pending) but adds significant complexity to the MVP. Deferred to Phase 2 or 3 depending on beta feedback. Reconsider after the single-file analysis workflow is proven and polished.

**Drag-and-drop DAW integration** — Dragging sounds from SampleMint directly into a DAW. This requires platform-specific drag-and-drop protocols that vary by OS and DAW. High value but high implementation risk. Deferred to post-MVP once the core sound design workflow is solid. Reconsider in Phase 3.

**Preset save/load/sharing** — Saving custom presets and loading others' presets. The MVP ships with built-in presets only. Adding user presets requires file format decisions, import/export UI, and potential compatibility concerns. Deferred to Phase 2. Reconsider immediately after MVP launch.

**Internationalization (i18n)** — Translation into the top 10 languages. Critical for the 6-month vision but adds scope to every UI element. Deferred until post-MVP when the UI is stable. Reconsider in Phase 4 or 5.

**Serial key licensing system** — The full license server, hardware fingerprinting, and Polar payment integration. The MVP ships as a free app (the freemium free tier). The licensing system is built in a dedicated phase once the product proves its value. Reconsider in Phase 3 or 4.

**Landing page and marketing site** — A public website for downloads and information. Not needed until the app is ready for public distribution. Deferred to the polish/launch phase.

### Feature Priority (MoSCoW)

**Must Have (P0):**
Audio file analysis (single file), preset library (core instruments), knob-based effects controls (reverb, echo, pitch, distortion, filter, volume), real-time waveform visualization, realistic vs. EDM mode toggle, A/B comparison, export to WAV/MP3, built-in file explorer, kit builder workspace, Electron cross-platform shell.

**Should Have (P1):**
Batch directory analysis, drag-and-drop DAW integration, preset save/load, additional instrument presets beyond the core set, keyboard shortcuts for common actions, undo/redo for knob adjustments.

**Could Have (P2):**
Internationalization (top 10 languages), preset sharing between users, custom export folder templates, audio file format support beyond WAV/MP3/FLAC (AIFF, OGG), waveform zoom and scroll, multiple effects chains.

**Won't Have (this release):**
In-app marketplace for selling sounds, cloud sync across devices, mobile companion app, MIDI controller support, VST/AU plugin format (SampleMint as a DAW plugin), collaborative editing, AI-assisted sound generation.

### Core User Flows

**Flow 1: Analyze and Recreate**
Trigger: Producer has a reference sound they want to create an original version of.
Steps: (1) Click "Analyze" or drag a file into the app. (2) File explorer opens, producer navigates to audio file. (3) SampleMint analyzes the file and displays waveform + analysis data. (4) Producer adjusts knobs (pitch, reverb, echo, distortion, filter). (5) Waveform updates in real time. (6) Producer clicks A/B to compare with original. (7) Producer exports the new sound as WAV.
Outcome: A new, original audio file on disk that sounds inspired by (but distinct from) the reference.
Success criteria: Complete flow in under 3 minutes. Exported file is high-quality. A/B shows clear differentiation.

**Flow 2: Design from Preset**
Trigger: Producer wants to create an original sound from scratch.
Steps: (1) Browse the preset library by instrument category. (2) Click a preset to load it — dry, realistic base sound plays and waveform displays. (3) Producer adjusts knobs and/or toggles EDM mode. (4) Waveform updates in real time. (5) Producer exports the sound.
Outcome: An original audio file designed from a preset base.
Success criteria: Complete flow in under 2 minutes. Preset sounds studio-quality. Adjustments are intuitive.

**Flow 3: Build a Kit**
Trigger: Producer has created multiple one-shots and wants to package them as a kit.
Steps: (1) Open the kit builder workspace. (2) Drag one-shots from the file explorer or recent exports into kit slots. (3) Name the kit. (4) Optionally reorder or remove sounds. (5) Export the kit as an organized folder.
Outcome: A named folder on disk containing organized one-shot files, ready to upload to BeatStars or similar.
Success criteria: Kit contains all expected files. Folder structure is clean. Total time under 2 minutes.

### Success Metrics

**Primary metric (North Star): Weekly active exporters** — unique users who export at least one sound per week. This measures real usage, not just downloads or opens. Good: 30 weekly exporters at 90 days. Great: 80 weekly exporters at 90 days.

**Secondary metrics:**
Downloads across all platforms — Good: 500 at 90 days. Great: 1,000 at 90 days.
Premium conversion rate — Good: 2% of total users. Great: 5% of total users.
Kits built per active user per week — Good: 1. Great: 3+.
Discord community members — Good: 100 at 90 days. Great: 300 at 90 days.

**Leading indicators:**
Time to first export (from first app launch) — Target: under 5 minutes. If this is high, the onboarding flow is failing.
Session duration — Target: 15–45 minutes. Too short means users aren't engaged. Too long might mean the workflow is slow.
Return rate within 7 days — Target: 40%+. Measures whether first use creates enough value to bring producers back.

### Risks

**Sound quality falls short (likelihood: medium, impact: critical).** If the preset library or the synthesis engine doesn't produce ultra-realistic, studio-quality sounds, nothing else matters. Producers will try it once and never return. Mitigation: Prototype the sound engine first, get producer feedback before building the UI, and invest heavily in preset quality over preset quantity.

**Audio processing library limitations (likelihood: medium, impact: high).** Leaning on existing libraries (Web Audio API, Tone.js, etc.) rather than building a custom synthesis engine means the output quality is constrained by those libraries' capabilities. Mitigation: Evaluate libraries early with specific quality benchmarks. Identify fallback options. Be prepared to supplement with custom DSP code for critical areas.

**Cross-platform Electron issues (likelihood: medium, impact: medium).** Audio handling, file system access, and drag-and-drop behave differently across macOS, Windows, and Linux. Mitigation: Test on all three platforms from day one. Prioritize Windows (largest producer user base) and macOS (second largest) over Linux for early debugging.

**Copyright positioning is hard to defend legally (likelihood: low, impact: high).** SampleMint's positioning is "sounds you create are copyrightable," but the legal reality of copyright for synthesized sounds is nuanced. If a competitor or lawyer challenges the claim, it could undermine the brand. Mitigation: Never claim to provide legal advice. Position as "you made the creative decisions, giving you the strongest possible copyright claim." Consult an IP attorney before launch for marketing language review.

**Free tier is too generous (likelihood: medium, impact: medium).** If the free tier gives producers everything they need, conversion to premium will be anemic. Mitigation: Gate the features that power users care about most — batch analysis, preset save/load, kit builder — behind premium. Monitor conversion rates and adjust the gate post-launch.

**Solo founder bandwidth (likelihood: high, impact: medium).** Mike is one person wearing every hat. Development, design, marketing, support, community management — the list is long and the hours are finite. Mitigation: Ruthlessly prioritize. The MVP should be genuinely minimal. Use AI coding tools to maximize development velocity. Build the Discord community early so that engaged users help with support and feedback.

**Market education required (likelihood: medium, impact: medium).** Many producers don't actively think about copyright when selling kits. The pain point is real, but latent — it may require education before it drives purchases. Mitigation: Content marketing that educates while it sells. YouTube demos that show the A/B comparison feature framed as "proof your sounds are yours." Let the copyright angle be a differentiator, not the only selling point — the speed and quality of the workflow should be compelling even for producers who don't care about copyright.

## 4. Brand Strategy

### Positioning Statement

For independent music producers and sound designers who want to create and sell original drum kits and one-shots, SampleMint is the sound design workstation that lets you analyze any reference, design from ultra-realistic presets, and export copyright-clear audio you fully own. Unlike AI generators and recycled sample packs, SampleMint puts every creative decision in the producer's hands, making every export legally and unmistakably theirs.

### Brand Personality

SampleMint is the underground expert — the producer in the studio who's been doing this for years and shows up with exactly what you need. Not loud, not flashy, not trying to impress anyone. Confident because the work speaks for itself. If SampleMint were a person, they'd be wearing all black, headphones around their neck, sitting in the corner of the studio adjusting levels while everyone else is talking. They'd answer your question in one sentence instead of five. They'd never use the word "synergy."

SampleMint talks to users like fellow producers, not like customers. There's no corporate distance, no upsell energy, no "Have you tried our premium features?" pop-ups mid-workflow. The respect is baked into the design: clean, dark, no clutter. Every pixel says "I know you're working. Let me get out of your way."

In different situations: during onboarding, SampleMint doesn't explain what a waveform is — it shows you one. During an error, it tells you what went wrong in five words. During a success, it confirms and moves on. During marketing, it shows the workflow, lets the sound quality speak, and lets producers sell producers.

### Voice & Tone Guide

**Voice (constant):** Direct, minimal, confident. Speaks in statements, not suggestions. Uses plain language, never jargon. Assumes the user is competent. Lets silence (white space) communicate as much as words.

### Brand Anti-Patterns

**Never use marketing language inside the product.** No "Unlock premium," no "Upgrade your experience," no exclamation marks in UI copy. If it sounds like it belongs on a landing page, it doesn't belong in the app. The upgrade path should be visible but never interruptive — a quiet settings menu item, not a pop-up.

**Never use stock photography or generic imagery.** No hands on a laptop. No "creative professional in a well-lit office." If imagery is used, it shows real studio setups, real audio waveforms, real interfaces. Better yet, use screenshots and product visuals only.

**Never gamify the experience.** No streak counts, no achievement badges, no progress bars for profile completion, no "You've exported 10 sounds!" celebrations. This is a professional tool. The reward is the sound, not a notification.

**Never use dense onboarding flows.** No 12-step tutorials. No "Did you know?" tooltips that pop up unsolicited. If the interface needs explaining, the interface is wrong. If a producer needs guidance, it should be available in a help section they choose to visit — not injected into their workflow.

**Never use words like "revolutionary," "game-changing," "cutting-edge," "next-gen," or "powered by AI."** SampleMint doesn't need superlatives because the product speaks for itself. And the explicit non-use of AI is a selling point, not a limitation.

## 5. Design Direction

### Design Philosophy

**Dark canvas, focused content.** The UI is a dark canvas — nearly black — that makes audio content (waveforms, active controls, selected presets) pop. This mirrors the environment producers actually work in: dimly lit studios where bright interfaces cause eye strain during long sessions.

**Every control earns its place.** No decorative elements. No controls that exist "just in case." If a knob is on screen, it does something the producer needs right now. White space isn't emptiness — it's clarity. A producer should be able to glance at the interface and immediately know what to do.

**Visual feedback is instant and honest.** When a producer turns a knob, the waveform responds before they finish the gesture. When they toggle between modes, the change is visible and audible simultaneously. The interface never lies about what the sound is doing.

**Professional restraint.** The lime green accent is used sparingly — for active states, waveforms, and primary actions. Everything else is shades of gray and black. The restraint is the aesthetic. One bold color against a dark canvas feels intentional and premium; multiple colors feel chaotic and amateurish.

### Visual Mood

Dark matte black base with vibrant lime green accents — the kind of interface that looks like it belongs on a producer's setup next to an Akai MPC or a set of studio monitors. The energy is "2 AM studio session" — focused, immersive, serious. Think the precision of Ableton's dark theme meets the swagger of a high-end VST plugin like Serum or FabFilter Pro-Q. Waveforms glow in lime green against the dark background. Active knobs and buttons pop with the accent color. Everything else recedes into quiet, dark surfaces that don't compete for attention. Typography is clean and modern — no serif fonts, no decorative type. The overall impression is: this tool means business.

### Color Palette

**Primary (Lime Green):** `#39FF14` — Used for waveforms, active knob states, primary action buttons, toggle indicators, and progress states. This is the signature color. Use it intentionally — it should always mean "active" or "important."

**Primary Hover:** `#32E612` — Slightly darker for hover states on interactive elements.

**Primary Muted:** `#39FF1433` — 20% opacity version for subtle backgrounds on active panels, selected list items.

**Background:** `#0A0A0A` — Near-black. The darkest surface — the app window background.

**Surface:** `#141414` — Slightly lighter than background. Used for panels, cards, sidebars, and contained areas.

**Surface Elevated:** `#1E1E1E` — For elements that sit above surfaces: dropdowns, modals, tooltips.

**Border:** `#2A2A2A` — Subtle dividers between panels. Never harsh — just enough to define boundaries.

**Text Primary:** `#E8E8E8` — Main body text and labels. Slightly off-white to reduce eye strain.

**Text Secondary:** `#888888` — Secondary information, placeholder text, inactive labels.

**Text Muted:** `#555555` — Tertiary info, disabled states.

**Success:** `#22C55E` — Export confirmation, valid states. Intentionally distinct from the lime green primary.

**Warning:** `#F59E0B` — Amber for caution states, unsaved changes indicators.

**Error:** `#EF4444` — Red for errors, destructive actions, failed exports.

**Info:** `#3B82F6` — Blue for informational states, tips (used sparingly).

CSS variable names: `--color-primary`, `--color-primary-hover`, `--color-primary-muted`, `--color-bg`, `--color-surface`, `--color-surface-elevated`, `--color-border`, `--color-text`, `--color-text-secondary`, `--color-text-muted`, `--color-success`, `--color-warning`, `--color-error`, `--color-info`.

### Typography

**Heading font:** Inter — Clean, modern sans-serif with excellent readability at all sizes. Professional without being corporate. Available on Google Fonts. Weights to load: 500 (Medium) for subheadings, 600 (Semibold) for headings, 700 (Bold) for display headings.

**Body font:** Inter — Same family for consistency. The type scale provides visual hierarchy without switching families. Weights: 400 (Regular) for body text, 500 (Medium) for emphasized text.

**Mono font:** JetBrains Mono — For technical displays: file paths, audio metadata, frequency values, sample rate info. Weight: 400 (Regular).

**Type scale:**
`--text-xs`: 0.75rem (12px) — Metadata, timestamps, fine print
`--text-sm`: 0.875rem (14px) — Secondary labels, file explorer items
`--text-base`: 1rem (16px) — Body text, primary labels, knob values
`--text-lg`: 1.125rem (18px) — Section headers, panel titles
`--text-xl`: 1.25rem (20px) — Page titles, kit names
`--text-2xl`: 1.5rem (24px) — Primary headings
`--text-3xl`: 1.875rem (30px) — Display headings (used sparingly)

**Line heights:**
Headings: 1.2 (tight, for impact)
Body: 1.5 (comfortable reading)
Mono: 1.4 (slightly tighter for technical data)

### Spacing & Layout

**Base unit:** 4px. All spacing uses multiples of 4.

**Spacing scale:**
`--space-1`: 4px — Inline padding, icon margins
`--space-2`: 8px — Tight spacing between related elements
`--space-3`: 12px — Default internal padding for small components
`--space-4`: 16px — Standard gap between elements, button padding
`--space-5`: 20px — Medium separation
`--space-6`: 24px — Section padding, panel internal margins
`--space-8`: 32px — Large gaps between sections
`--space-10`: 40px — Major section separation
`--space-12`: 48px — Panel separation
`--space-16`: 64px — Page-level padding

**Max content width:** 1440px — optimized for typical producer monitor sizes. The app is full-width by default as an Electron window.

**Layout grid:** The main interface is a 3-column layout: File Explorer (left, 240px default width, resizable), Main Workspace (center, flexible), and Kit Builder/Properties (right, 280px default width, collapsible). The waveform display occupies the upper portion of the main workspace. The knob controls sit below the waveform.

**Responsive behavior:** As an Electron app, the primary concern is window resizing, not mobile breakpoints. Minimum window size: 1024×768. Below that, the sidebar panels collapse to icons. The waveform display and knob controls always remain visible.

### Component Philosophy

**Border radius:** Minimal and consistent. `--radius-sm`: 4px for small elements (badges, tags). `--radius-md`: 6px for buttons, inputs, and cards. `--radius-lg`: 8px for panels and modals. `--radius-full`: 9999px for circular elements (knobs, toggle indicators). No rounded corners larger than 8px — the aesthetic is precise, not bubbly.

**Shadows:** Used sparingly. Only for elevated elements: modals, dropdowns, tooltips. Shadow color is always black at low opacity. `--shadow-sm`: `0 1px 2px rgba(0,0,0,0.3)`. `--shadow-md`: `0 4px 12px rgba(0,0,0,0.4)`. `--shadow-lg`: `0 8px 24px rgba(0,0,0,0.5)`. Panels and cards use borders for definition, not shadows.

**Borders:** 1px solid `--color-border` for panel edges and dividers. Never thicker than 1px for structural borders. Active/selected states use `--color-primary` borders at 1px.

**Buttons:** Two variants only. Primary: lime green background (`--color-primary`), dark text, used for primary actions (Export, Analyze, Save Kit). Secondary: transparent background with `--color-border` border, text in `--color-text`, used for secondary actions. No outline variants, no ghost buttons, no link-style buttons. Hover darkens primary 10%, lightens secondary background slightly.

**Inputs:** Dark surface background (`--color-surface-elevated`), subtle border, text in `--color-text`. Focus state adds `--color-primary` border. No labels above inputs unless necessary — use placeholder text where appropriate.

**Knobs:** Circular, rendered as rotary controls with a position indicator line. Inactive: `--color-surface-elevated` with `--color-border` ring. Active/adjusting: ring and indicator line in `--color-primary` with subtle glow. Value displayed beneath or beside the knob in `--text-sm` mono font.

### Iconography & Imagery

**Icon style:** Outline style with 1.5px stroke weight. Clean, minimal, functional. Consistent with the "professional restraint" philosophy.

**Recommended library:** Lucide icons — open source, consistent stroke weight, extensive set covering audio and file management concepts. Use at 20px default size, 16px in tight spaces.

**Audio-specific icons:** Waveform icon for analysis, play/pause/stop for audio controls, folder for file explorer, download/export arrow for export actions. Knob icons should be custom-rendered SVGs matching the actual knob component style.

**Illustration style:** None. SampleMint does not use illustrations. Visual communication comes from the product itself — waveforms, knob states, audio visualizations. If explanatory visuals are needed (e.g., for a help section), use annotated screenshots.

**What to avoid:** No 3D renders. No gradients on icons. No emoji in the interface. No decorative imagery. No product screenshots with fake/mocked data that doesn't represent real usage.

### Accessibility Commitments

**WCAG target:** 2.1 AA compliance.

**Color contrast:** All text meets 4.5:1 contrast ratio against its background. The lime green primary (`#39FF14`) on dark backgrounds exceeds this. Interactive elements have distinct focus, hover, and active states beyond color alone.

**Keyboard navigation:** Every interactive element is reachable via Tab. Knob controls are adjustable via arrow keys. Keyboard shortcuts for primary actions: Ctrl/Cmd+E (export), Ctrl/Cmd+A (analyze), Space (play/pause audio preview). Focus indicators are visible and use `--color-primary` with a 2px offset outline.

**Screen reader support:** All interactive elements have descriptive aria-labels. Waveform visualization includes text alternatives describing the audio characteristics. Knob values are announced on change.

**Minimum touch targets:** 44×44px for all clickable elements. Knobs have generous hit areas — at least 48×48px.

**Motion sensitivity:** Users can disable waveform animation via a settings toggle. Reduced motion preference is respected via `prefers-reduced-motion` media query.

### Motion & Interaction

**Default transition duration:** 150ms for micro-interactions (hover, focus). 200ms for state changes (panel open/close). 300ms for major transitions (view switching). Never exceed 300ms — speed is a core value.

**Easing curve:** `cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)` — fast start, smooth deceleration. Feels responsive and intentional.

**What animates:** Hover/focus/active states on buttons and knobs. Waveform rendering (real-time). Panel collapse/expand. Dropdown open/close. A/B comparison toggle (crossfade between waveforms).

**What does not animate:** Page content appearing on load (instant). File list population (instant). Export (instant confirmation, no progress animation for sub-second operations). Knob value labels (instant update).

**Loading states:** For operations under 200ms, no loading indicator. For 200ms–2s (file analysis), show a minimal pulse animation on the waveform area in `--color-primary` at 20% opacity. For 2s+ (batch operations in future), show a subtle progress bar at the top of the workspace.
